My Soccer Journey: From Dusty Roads to Community Glory

I started playing soccer when I was just a young child. Back then, the ball wasn’t the professional kind you see today — it was made from plastic papers carefully tied together. We didn’t have fancy soccer shoes either; most of the time, we played barefoot or in whatever shoes we had. Our “field” was often the wide, dusty roads of my village, where cars were a rare sight. Instead, donkey carts and cattle-driven wagons were the usual passersby, making the space both challenging and adventurous.

Playing in those dusty roads meant we ended each game covered from head to toe in fine red dust. I still carry a mark on my toenail — a painful black bruise from kicking the hard ground too fiercely once. That pain lasted for nearly two weeks, but it never kept me off the field. Soccer wasn’t just a game; it was a passion that burned too bright to let a little pain stop me.

There was a clever ritual we followed to hide the evidence of our playtime from my grandparents — we’d remove our shirts before going home so they wouldn’t see the dirt and dust. I usually wore two layers of shorts; the bottom layer was my “clean” pair, and I would strip off the top one right before the match began.

Our games had no referees or formal rules. We played until the owner of the ball decided it was time to stop or until the sky grew too dark to see each other. Sometimes, we split into teams of five or three, depending on how many players showed up. When many of us gathered, we even made three or four teams and played knockout-style tournaments. The ball’s owner was the sole judge — only they could call goals, penalties, or fouls. If teams were uneven, the smaller team would pick players from the other side to even things out. It was raw, fair, and full of joy.

After the game, our first stop was always the nearest river. Without soap or towels, we splashed water over our dusty faces and bodies, washing off the grime of the day. Then we’d dash home, drying off under the sun before putting on our shirts and clean shorts again. My day wasn’t over after soccer — I’d head straight to the hillside to untie and herd my goats back home. Sometimes, I arrived late, and my grandparents would be worried or even angry, especially when darkness had fallen.

Despite all this, soccer was never far from my heart. Through primary and high school, even though I focused on my studies, I was known as a strong player. I rarely missed a practice and was often chosen to play in matches. I played for different clubs, leading my teams to finals and winning trophies. I was proud to receive individual awards like Man of the Match, Player of the Season, and Top Goal Scorer — each trophy a testament to the hard work and love I poured into the game.

Moving to the USA didn’t change my passion. I joined a local community club, where I still play regularly. I make it a point to contribute in every game by scoring, living by my motto: “Whether we win, lose, or draw, there must be my goal.” This dedication has earned me the respect and friendship of teammates and coaches alike.

Soccer taught me resilience, teamwork, and joy — values that have shaped who I am today. It’s more than just a sport; it’s a lifelong journey filled with memories, friendships, and dreams.
